# Report

## Executive Summary

**Market gap.** I did not find a product that already combines all four target properties: Photos-grade mobile UX, multiple independently password-keyed encrypted galleries, serverless/local-first peer sync, and provider-agnostic zero-knowledge collaboration. Ente comes closest on encrypted photo UX and sharing: its [architecture document](https://github.com/ente-io/ente/blob/main/architecture/README.md) describes per-file keys wrapped by collection keys, collection keys wrapped by a master key, Argon2-derived key encryption, libsodium primitives, and album sharing by encrypting the collection key to a recipient public key. Ente’s [public links](https://ente.com/help/photos/features/sharing-and-collaboration/public-links) also implement a useful invite pattern: album decryption keys are placed in the URL fragment, collect links can accept uploads from non-users, and passwords are server-side access controls rather than re-encryption. The missing pieces are independently password-keyed multi-vault galleries and serverless Multipeer sync; Ente remains account/server-oriented, even though its [self-hosting guide](https://github.com/ente-io/ente/blob/main/docs/docs/self-hosting/index.md) makes the server open and deployable.

**Prior-art landscape.** Stingle is strong cryptographic prior art for an encrypted photo app: its [security whitepaper](https://stingle.org/security/) describes XChaCha20-Poly1305 file encryption, X25519 public-key crypto, Argon2id key derivation, and album sharing by re-encrypting an album private key to the recipient’s public key without touching file blobs. Proton Drive Photos contributes a mature hierarchical sharing model: Proton’s [security model](https://proton.me/blog/protondrive-security) uses volumes, shares, per-node keys/passphrases, signed encrypted blocks, and URL-password sharing where the password is not sent to Proton; Proton’s album sharing docs add Viewer and Editor roles, but editors can remove photos from an album, so it does not match the proposed “add but not delete others’ content” rule. Apple Photos with Advanced Data Protection is a major UX incumbent but a weak comparator for this collaboration model, because Apple’s [iCloud overview](https://support.apple.com/en-us/102651) says Photos can be end-to-end encrypted under ADP, while Shared Albums and “anyone with the link” sharing are not end-to-end encrypted.

**Adjacent systems clarify what not to build.** Cryptomator is the cleanest multi-vault reference: each vault has its own master keys, a scrypt-derived key-encryption key, AES Key Wrap, and a virtual filesystem interface in its [security architecture](https://docs.cryptomator.org/security/architecture/), while Cryptomator Hub shows zero-knowledge key brokering for shared vault access. But Cryptomator is file UX rather than photo UX, so it is prior art for vault boundaries and KDF/key wrapping rather than for gallery interaction. Immich and PhotoPrism are useful negative examples: Immich’s [developer architecture](https://github.com/immich-app/immich/blob/main/docs/docs/developer/architecture.mdx) is a server, Postgres, Redis, and machine-learning-service design with thumbnails, metadata extraction, facial recognition, and transcoding jobs, while PhotoPrism’s setup docs warn that files and passwords can be transmitted in clear text without HTTPS and its community discussions frame encryption as storage-layer or backup tooling rather than browser/client E2EE. These projects deliver excellent self-hosted photo management, but the server must process plaintext or derived media representations to provide search, thumbnails, indexing, and ML.

**Consumer vault apps validate UX but not the whole security claim.** Keepsafe documents AES-256 at-rest encryption and Premium per-album passwords, and its [album-lock guide](https://support.getkeepsafe.com/hc/en-us/articles/360035401091-Locking-unlocking-albums-with-a-password) directly supports the “extra password per album” UX pattern. Private Photo Vault documents AES-256, local-only mode, encrypted Cloud Vault, decoy passwords, and intruder capture in its [product FAQ](https://privatephotovault.com/faq/). 2Stable’s Photo Vault claims zero-knowledge, password-derived E2EE, local storage, cross-device sync, and irrecoverable passwords in its [product page](https://photovault.2stable.com/), though the page now indicates the product is end-of-life. Encamera’s [App Store listing](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/encamera-encrypted-photo-vault/id1639202616) claims local or personal iCloud storage, no account, direct encrypted capture, open-source transparency, and album-by-album storage choice, making it the closest consumer UX neighbor for local-first encrypted capture, but it does not publicly evidence multi-party zero-knowledge collaboration or local P2P sync.

**Failure modes and audit signals.** Ente is unusually well documented: a Cure53 [crypto audit](https://cure53.de/audit-report_ente-crypto.pdf) found correct libsodium and Argon2 usage but flagged weak password policy, key-retention after password change, encrypted master-key exposure via email compromise, and share-revocation limits; a later Cure53 [platform audit](https://ente.com/reports/Cure53-Audit-Report-Oct-2025.pdf) reported issues including replay protection, Cloudflare/self-hosting IP assumptions, stored XSS, SSRF, and user-enumeration paths. Cryptomator’s Cure53 [crypto review](https://cryptomator.org/audits/2017-11-27%20crypto%20cure53.pdf) was favorable overall but found a public release-signing private key and an AES/ECB default in a crypto adapter that was not judged to undermine the vault model. The broader Android vault category has a poor historical security record: a forensic study of 18 vault apps reported that several did not encrypt photos or videos, some stored passwords in cleartext, and some were vulnerable to file-swap attacks, which is a warning against opaque “photo vault” claims without open specs, test vectors, and external review.

## Conclusion

**No incumbent fully matches the proposed combination.** The nearest photo-native zero-knowledge competitors are Ente and Stingle: Ente has polished E2EE photo UX, collection keys, collaborative albums, collect links, self-hosting, and CLI/export tooling, while Stingle has album-key sharing and open-source mobile clients. But both still use a service/server sync plane rather than serverless local Multipeer sync, and their vault unit is not an independently password-keyed, Cryptomator-like set of multiple galleries. Cryptomator proves that users accept multiple independently unlocked vaults, and its [vault architecture](https://docs.cryptomator.org/security/architecture/) is the clearest prior art for per-vault keys, but it lacks Photos-grade browsing, albums, thumbnails, background import, and collaboration UX. Consumer vaults such as Keepsafe, Private Photo Vault, 2Stable Photo Vault, Vaulty-style apps, and Encamera validate demand for PIN/Face ID vault UX and per-album locks, but public evidence generally shows weaker or less transparent cryptographic architecture than Ente, Stingle, Proton, and Cryptomator.

**The product opportunity is a local-first, capability-scoped encrypted photo system.** The design should adopt Ente-style collection/file key hierarchy and link-fragment public sharing, Stingle-style fast album sharing by rewrapping an album private key, Cryptomator-style multiple vault identities and independent unlock durations, and Proton-style explicit Viewer/Editor roles. The differentiator should be stronger than Proton’s current editor model: collaborators may add their own encrypted objects but cannot delete others’ objects, which implies append-only object ownership, signed mutation logs, tombstones scoped to the object owner or gallery owner, and conflict handling designed for offline peers. The first CLI/macOS/Linux peer should be treated as a headless sync device with its own device key, not as a trusted server, so the app remains provider-agnostic even if users later park encrypted blobs on iCloud Drive, SMB, Syncthing, S3, or removable storage.
